## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Tampa

**Check Florida licensure first.** Every surveyor in Florida must be a Licensed Professional Surveyor (PLS) and carry a current, verifiable license from the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation. Call the DBPR or check their database online—takes two minutes, saves you from unlicensed operators. Don't assume the listing means the license is current.

**Ask what survey type you actually need.** Most people say "I need a boundary survey" when they might need an ALTA/NSPS title survey (required by title companies and lenders, includes more detail, costs more), an elevation certificate (flood insurance), or just a mortgage survey (cheaper, narrower scope). A qualified surveyor should ask clarifying questions—not just quote you the first price.

**Verify their local experience.** Tampa's geography matters: limestone geology affects staking, coastal properties bring elevation and storm surge questions, and the Hillsborough County Clerk records can be a maze. A surveyor who's worked Tampa projects for years will navigate county records, zoning issues, and deed quirks faster than someone from out of state.

**Get references from title companies, real estate attorneys, or your lender.** These folks hire surveyors every week. They know who delivers on time, who communicates clearly, and who corners-cuts. One phone call to a title company usually nets you three solid names.

> **Pro Tip:** Don't just compare prices. A $600 boundary survey from a rush job done by someone juggling five projects plays out differently than a $800 survey from someone who spends an afternoon getting it right. Faster and cheaper aren't the same as better.

## What to Expect

A simple residential boundary survey in Tampa typically runs $500–$1,200, depending on property size, record complexity, and how buried the original monuments are. ALTA/NSPS surveys (required for commercial transactions and mortgages) run $2,500–$5,000+ because they include way more detail—easements, improvements, encroachments, flood zones. Topographic and construction staking surveys depend entirely on site size and complexity.

Turnaround is usually 1–3 weeks for standard residential work, longer for ALTA or complex boundary disputes. The surveyor will need deeds, mortgage documents, previous surveys if you have them, and site access. They'll set monuments or confirm existing ones, take measurements, and deliver a sealed and signed plat (the legal document that bears the surveyor's professional seal and license number—this is what makes it official).

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote isn't always the low-ball. Sometimes it is. Sometimes it means they're understaffed or cutting corners on research. Ask turnaround time and scope in the same conversation you ask price. That context matters.
