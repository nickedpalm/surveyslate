# Land Surveyors in [Wilmington](/locations/delaware/wilmington/), Delaware

Finding a qualified land surveyor in Wilmington isn't like hiring a contractor—you can't just skim reviews and call the cheapest option. A bad survey can derail a real estate closing, tank a property dispute, or leave you liable for boundary errors that cost tens of thousands to fix. The problem is that most people don't know what they're actually buying until they're already committed.

## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Wilmington

**Check licensure first.** Delaware licenses Professional Land Surveyors (PLS) through the Division of Professional Regulation. Verify the surveyor's active license on the state website before you call. This isn't optional—anyone without a current PLS license isn't legally allowed to sign and seal survey documents in Delaware. If they can't prove it, move on.

**Ask what survey type you need.** The term "land survey" covers six completely different deliverables. A boundary survey ($500–$1,500 for a residential property) answers "where are my property lines?" An ALTA/NSPS title survey ($2,000–$5,000+) goes deeper and includes easements, encroachments, and improvements—required by most lenders and title companies for commercial transactions. Topographic surveys, construction staking, and elevation certificates are separate beasts entirely. Don't let a surveyor upsell you. Tell them your exact use case, and they'll quote you correctly.

**Verify experience with your property type.** A surveyor who specializes in residential subdivisions in New Castle County may not be your best choice if you're surveying an industrial lot in downtown Wilmington with old deed language or complex easement history. Wilmington's downtown properties often have dense, overlapping claims and historic ownership quirks. You want someone who's navigated that specific chaos before.

**Get the timeline in writing.** Standard boundary surveys take 1–2 weeks. ALTA surveys can take 3–4 weeks because they require title searches and coordination with the title company. Don't assume. Ask upfront, get it in an email, and confirm they can hit your closing date. Surveyors in the tri-state area get slammed during spring and summer—book early if you're on a deadline.

> **Pro Tip:** Delaware allows surveyors to be licensed in multiple states. If your property straddles a Delaware-Pennsylvania boundary or you're working with multi-state developers, ask whether your surveyor holds licenses in neighboring states. It saves coordination headaches and reduces the chance of conflicting survey data.

## What to Expect

You'll pay $500–$1,500 for a straightforward residential boundary survey, $2,000–$5,000 for an ALTA survey on a commercial property, and more if there are complications—old deeds with vague language, underground utilities that need locating, or boundary disputes that require research. The process is straightforward: you provide legal description and property access, the surveyor conducts fieldwork, reviews title and deed history, and delivers a signed and sealed document within the quoted timeline. Turnaround is typically 1–4 weeks depending on complexity.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote isn't your best deal. A surveyor who underbids likely either cuts corners (incomplete research, rushed fieldwork) or low-balls to upsell add-ons. If a quote is 30% below market, ask why. Get three quotes and compare scope, not just price.
