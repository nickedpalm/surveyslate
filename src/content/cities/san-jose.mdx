## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in San Jose

**1. Verify California Licensure First**
California requires all surveyors to hold a PLS (Professional Land Surveyor) license issued by the State Board for Professional Engineers, Land Surveyors, and Geologists. Don't assume someone calling themselves a "surveyor" is licensed—check their license number against the state database. An expired or restricted license is a red flag that kills the entire relationship.

**2. Match the Survey Type to Their Expertise**
A surveyor who's done 200 boundary surveys might be slow on ALTA/NSPS title work (the kind lenders and title companies demand for commercial real estate). Ask directly: "How many [your survey type] surveys have you completed in the last two years?" Recent, relevant work beats a long résumé.

**3. Ask About Local Experience in Santa Clara County**
San Jose's mix of historic downtown parcels, sprawling residential suburbs, and tech-corridor development means boundary records vary wildly by neighborhood. A surveyor who knows where the old county records are buried and understands local title quirks will save you weeks and headaches. Bonus: they'll spot issues before they become expensive surprises.

**4. Get a Written Scope and Timeline**
Before you sign anything, get a detailed quote that spells out what's included (site visit, research, deliverables, number of revisions) and how long it'll take. "5-7 business days" is solid for residential boundary work; ALTA surveys often run 10-14 days depending on title complexity.

**Pro Tip:** Call three surveyors, not one. You'll hear the same two or three issues flagged across all three calls—that's real market intel. The outlier quote (too cheap or way over) is usually a signal something's off.

## What to Expect

A residential boundary survey typically costs $500–$1,500, depending on lot size and accessibility. ALTA/NSPS surveys for commercial or mortgage work run $2,000–$5,000+ because they demand exhaustive title research, easement hunting, and detailed staking. Turnaround is usually 1–2 weeks for routine work; add time if the surveyor needs to pull old county records or resolve boundary ambiguities with neighbors.

The deliverable is a sealed, signed drawing—your surveyor stakes their professional reputation on every line. That seal matters: it's what makes the survey admissible in court and acceptable to lenders, title companies, and municipalities.

> **Reality Check:** The cheapest quote often means corners cut—incomplete title research, rushed fieldwork, or a surveyor juggling too many jobs. Mid-range pricing ($1,000–$2,500 for residential) usually signals someone invested in the work.
