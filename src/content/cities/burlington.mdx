## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Burlington

**Check their Vermont PLS license.** Every surveyor in the state must be a Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) and carry a current license. Verify this on the Vermont Division of Professional Regulation website. It takes 30 seconds and eliminates the wrong people immediately. A licensed surveyor signs and seals their deliverables — that seal has legal weight. Without it, the document is essentially decorative.

**Ask what type of survey you need — then ask them what that means.** A boundary survey for a residential property runs $500–$1,500. An ALTA/NSPS title survey (required by most title companies and lenders for commercial transactions) costs $1,500–$5,000+. A topographic survey for construction or engineering adds complexity and cost. If a surveyor quotes you the same price for all three, they're either guessing or not being straight with you. The right firm should ask *you* questions first: What's the property size? What's the intended use? Is this for a sale, refinance, or dispute resolution? Their answer tells you whether they actually understand your situation.

**Go local when the stakes are high.** Burlington real estate law, municipal regulations, and deed records have specific quirks. A surveyor who's spent years in Chittenden County knows where the boundary disputes typically flare up, which town records are poorly indexed, and which easements tend to be buried in the fine print. For a straightforward residential boundary survey, this matters less. For anything touching commercial development or a contested line, local expertise is worth the premium.

**Pro Tip:** Ask for references from title companies or attorneys who've hired them. If a surveyor is trusted by the people who stake their reputation on survey accuracy, that's not a coincidence.

## What to Expect

Most boundary surveys take 2–4 weeks from contract to final deliverable, depending on property complexity and how quickly you can provide deed information. Turnaround speeds up if you have clean title documents and clear access to the property. ALTA surveys take longer because they involve more research and title company requirements. Expect to pay upfront — typically 50% deposit, balance upon completion.

**Reality Check:** Don't confuse a low quote with a good deal. If surveyor A charges $800 and surveyor B charges $1,200 for the same property, the difference usually isn't cut corners — it's methodology, experience, or a misunderstanding of scope. The cheapest bid is often the one that didn't fully understand your project.

## Local Market Overview

Burlington sits in Chittenden County, home to a mix of historic residential properties, commercial real estate, and increasingly complex development projects around the waterfront and downtown core. Municipal regulations here are tighter than in rural Vermont, which means surveys for residential work often involve checking zoning compliance and setback requirements. For anyone buying or developing property in the city, having a surveyor who knows Burlington's specific regulatory landscape — and can communicate with city planning — makes the process measurably smoother.
