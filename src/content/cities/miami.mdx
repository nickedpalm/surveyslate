# Land Surveyors in [Miami](/locations/florida/miami/), Florida

Finding a qualified land surveyor in Miami is harder than it should be—you're juggling title companies who'll recommend whoever picks up fastest, online reviews that don't tell you if someone actually knows South Florida property law, and the constant low-level anxiety that you've hired someone who'll miss a boundary issue and cost you six figures down the line. The directory below cuts through that. It's built around one principle: a licensed PLS (Professional Land Surveyor) who signs and seals their work in Florida, knows the local quirks (saltwater intrusion, drainage easements, pre-1970s platting inconsistencies), and has real credentials—not just a website.

## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Miami

- **Verify Florida licensure first.** This isn't optional. Check the Florida Board of Professional Surveyors and Mapmakers. If they're not listed, they're not legal to sign surveys in the state. Period. Anyone asking you to hire them without this verification is a red flag.

- **Ask for ALTA/NSPS experience if you're buying commercial or investment property.** Not all residential boundary surveys are created equal. If a title company is breathing down your neck, your surveyor needs to know ALTA/NSPS standards cold—these are the surveys that satisfy lenders and title insurance carriers. A surveyor who knows residential work but fumbles commercial is going to cost you closing delays.

- **Understand Miami's specific survey challenges.** South Florida's drainage patterns, historical platting (especially in older neighborhoods), and the prevalence of canal-adjacent properties create complexity that a surveyor from, say, Tallahassee might not immediately clock. Ask prospective surveyors about their experience with tidal influence on boundary markers and riparian rights. Their answer tells you if they've done this work before.

- **Get the timeline and fee in writing.** A simple residential boundary survey runs $500–$1,200 in Miami. ALTA surveys, subdivision platting, and complex commercial work? $2,000–$5,000+. Don't let anyone quote you over the phone without seeing the property first. Ground conditions, record complexity, and access all move the needle. Written estimates protect you and them.

- **Ask for references from title companies or real estate attorneys, not just homeowners.** A homeowner can tell you if someone showed up on time; an attorney or title officer can tell you if the surveyor caught a boundary issue that saved a transaction. The second conversation is the one that matters.

> **Pro Tip:** If you're in a multi-unit property or condo, verify that your surveyor has experience with vertical surveys and condominium documentation. Not all surveyors do this work, and it requires a different skill set.

## What to Expect

A boundary survey typically takes 2–4 weeks from start to finish in Miami—initial site visit, record research, field work, and final platting. Turnaround can shrink to one week if you're paying for expedited work and the surveyor has available capacity. ALTA surveys and complex topographic work take longer.

Your surveyor will show up with GPS equipment, a metal detector (to hunt for old markers), and—if it's a serious job—a drone for aerial reference. They're measuring, photographing, and cross-referencing public records. The deliverable is a sealed and signed survey document that title companies, lenders, and courts will accept. That seal is legal weight; it's why licensing matters.

> **Reality Check:** You're not just paying for the fieldwork—you're paying for the surveyor's liability insurance, their Florida education and licensing, and the fact that their signature on the document carries legal weight. Trying to save money by hiring unlicensed help or a junior technician without PLS backing is how you end up with a survey a lender won't accept and no legal recourse when something goes wrong.
