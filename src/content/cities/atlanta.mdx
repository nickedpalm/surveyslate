## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Atlanta

**Verify licensure first.** Every surveyor here is Georgia-licensed and seals their own work—that signature and stamp mean they're personally liable for accuracy. Check the [Georgia Secretary of State](https://sos.ga.gov) if you want to confirm credentials independently. It takes 30 seconds and eliminates imposters.

**Match the survey type to your need.** Buying a house? You likely need a boundary survey ($500–$1,500 depending on lot size and complexity). Refinancing? The lender specifies what they want—often a mortgage survey or elevation certificate. Developing commercial land or settling a boundary dispute? You're looking at ALTA/NSPS work ($2,000–$5,000+) or specialized topographic surveys. Ask the surveyor directly what *you* need before agreeing to anything.

**Ask about turnaround and availability.** Atlanta's market doesn't wait. A reputable firm will give you a realistic timeline upfront—usually 3–10 business days for standard residential work—and won't oversell availability just to land the job. If they promise 48 hours on a complex survey, that's a red flag.

**Confirm insurance and multi-state licensing.** If your property is near a county line or your development spans jurisdictions, you want someone licensed in multiple states. Always ask about Errors & Omissions (E&O) insurance; it protects you if something goes wrong.

**Pro Tip:** Call the surveyor directly, not just their office. You want to know if they understand *your specific situation*. A 10-minute conversation will tell you whether they've handled work like yours before. Professionals take that call.

## What to Expect

Most residential surveys in Atlanta run $600–$1,500; commercial ALTA work and complex boundary cases push toward $2,000–$5,000+. Turnaround is typically 5–10 business days once the surveyor's in the field, though rush jobs are possible if you're paying premium rates. The process is straightforward: you provide the legal description and property address, the surveyor researches previous surveys and deeds, stakes or marks the corners, measures distances and bearings, and delivers a signed, sealed survey document (usually a PDF and paper copy). Title companies and lenders accept sealed deliverables; you're buying certainty and legal liability.

**Reality Check:** Don't confuse a cheap quote with a good deal. Lowball pricing often means the surveyor is cutting corners—fewer field hours, less research, weaker client communication. You're paying for accuracy and their license on the line, not just their time.
