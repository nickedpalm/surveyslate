# Land Surveyors in [Honolulu](/locations/hawaii/honolulu/), Hawaii

You need a land surveyor in Honolulu. Maybe you're buying a property on the North Shore and the title company flagged some boundary questions. Maybe you're a developer dealing with a subdivision that predates modern surveys. Or maybe you inherited a plot and want to know exactly what you own before the lawyers get involved. The problem: finding someone who's actually licensed in Hawaii, knows the local quirks (deed descriptions written in the 1950s don't always match reality here), and won't charge you $3,000 for a $1,200 job.

## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Honolulu

**Verify Hawaii licensure first.** Every surveyor's seal and signature matters legally—but only if they're actually licensed in Hawaii. The Hawaii Board of Registration for Professional Engineers, Architects, Surveyors, and Landscape Architects maintains the official roster. If they're not on it, they can't legally sign your survey. Period.

**Check what type of work they actually do.** A surveyor who specializes in construction staking on Oahu commercial projects may not be your best fit for a residential boundary survey on a rural Maui property they've never worked before. Look at their portfolio—are they doing ALTA/NSPS work for title companies? Boundary disputes? Topographic surveys? Match the firm's experience to your actual need.

**Ask about local knowledge.** Honolulu's property records are solid, but Hawaii's deed descriptions often reference landmarks, old sugar plantation lines, or oral tradition. A surveyor who's worked the islands for 10+ years will know which county assessor quirks to anticipate and which boundary disputes tend to flare up in specific neighborhoods.

**Confirm they're NSPS members or hold additional certifications.** Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) licensure is mandatory. NSPS membership, CST (Certified Survey Technician) credentials, or multi-state licensing shows they're serious about standards and continuing education. It's not everything—but it filters out the corner-cutters.

> **Pro Tip:** Get a written estimate before they start. Survey fees range wildly ($500 for a simple residential boundary to $5,000+ for complex commercial or ALTA work), and the difference usually comes down to property size, deed complexity, and how much fieldwork the site requires. A firm that can't estimate upfront is a red flag.

## What to Expect

A basic residential boundary survey in Honolulu typically runs $800–$1,500 and takes 7–14 days from contract to sealed deliverable. ALTA/NSPS surveys (required by most title companies) are more thorough and run $2,000–$4,500 depending on the parcel. Commercial or subdivision work goes higher. Every surveyor signs and seals their work—that seal is what makes it legally binding.

Turnaround depends on county records availability. Honolulu County records are generally accessible, so you're not waiting weeks for documents. But if the property has complex history, old disputes, or liens that need research, add time.

> **Reality Check:** Don't confuse the survey cost with the *total* cost of a transaction. A $1,200 survey is not expensive in the context of a $500,000 real estate deal. Cutting corners here to save $300 is how you end up in a boundary dispute five years later that costs $15,000 to litigate.
