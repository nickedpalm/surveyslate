# Land Surveyors in [Salt Lake City](/locations/utah/salt-lake-city/), Utah

You need a surveyor, and you need one who won't disappear for six weeks or hand you a plat that your title company rejects two days before closing. In Salt Lake City—where real estate moves fast and the Wasatch Front keeps getting denser—finding a *qualified* surveyor fast enough to keep deals on track is harder than it should be. This directory cuts through the noise.

## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Salt Lake City

**Verify licensure first.** Utah's Department of Professional Licensing maintains a searchable database of licensed Professional Land Surveyors (PLS). Your surveyor must be licensed in Utah and should carry errors & omissions (E&O) insurance—non-negotiable. If they don't mention it unprompted, that's a yellow flag.

**Ask what survey type you actually need.** Boundary surveys are the baseline ($500–$1,500 for residential). ALTA/NSPS surveys (required by many title companies for commercial transactions) run $2,500–$5,000+ because they're legally rigorous and take longer. Topographic surveys, elevation certificates, and construction staking each have different timelines and costs. A good surveyor will tell you what you need, not just what you asked for.

**Turnaround matters locally.** Salt Lake County moves fast. Reputable surveyors here book out 2–4 weeks in peak season (spring/summer). If someone promises delivery in three days, they're either cutting corners or not actually the one doing the work. Ask upfront about their queue and get a firm deadline in writing.

**Check their footprint in Utah courts.** If they've done expert testimony or work in your specific neighborhood, they'll have context about local boundary disputes, easement issues, or topography quirks that can save you headaches. NSPS membership is a good sign—it means they subscribe to professional ethics and continuing education.

**Pro Tip:** Call your title company before hiring a surveyor. Ask them who they *don't* work with. Title companies see bad surveys constantly and have strong opinions about local practitioners. That intel is gold.

## What to Expect

A simple residential boundary survey takes 1–2 weeks once the surveyor schedules the field work. They'll visit your property, locate existing monuments or set new ones, measure the boundary, and deliver a sealed plat. ALTA surveys are slower (2–4 weeks) because they require title research, extended boundary work, and documented inspections for easements, encroachments, and utilities.

Pricing breaks down like this: $500–$1,500 for standard boundary work on a single lot; $2,000–$5,000+ for ALTA/NSPS or multi-parcel commercial surveys. Rush jobs cost more. Difficult terrain, missing monuments, or tangled title histories add time and money.

**Reality Check:** The cheapest quote isn't the safest deal. A $200 survey doesn't exist—if someone offers it, they're not actually surveying. Conversely, paying $8,000 for a residential boundary survey means you didn't shop around. Get three quotes, compare scope, and trust the middle ground.

## Local Market Overview

Salt Lake City's real estate market is competitive and title-conscious. Most transactions involve ALTA surveys or at least a mortgage survey, which means your surveyor needs to understand Utah Title Company standards and work well with local lenders. The city's expanding footprint also means more construction surveys and stake-outs for subdivision platting—surveyors who understand Utah County and Salt Lake County regulations move faster.
