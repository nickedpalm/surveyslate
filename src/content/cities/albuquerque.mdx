## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Albuquerque

**Look for a PLS license and current New Mexico state registration.** Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) licensure is non-negotiable — it's the only credential that matters legally. Every survey that counts (ALTA/NSPS for real estate, boundary work for title insurance, anything that might end up in court) must be signed and sealed by a licensed PLS. Check the New Mexico Environment Department's Professional Board records if you're unsure.

**Ask what survey type you actually need.** Most homeowners think "boundary survey," but the price and scope differ wildly. A simple residential boundary survey runs $500–$1,500 if your property is under 2 acres with clear records. ALTA/NSPS title surveys (the gold standard for commercial deals and mortgage lenders) cost $1,500–$5,000+ because they're exhaustive — they include title research, utility locates, easement verification, and ten times the detail. Topographic surveys and construction staking are separate animals entirely. A good surveyor will ask about your actual problem before quoting.

**Verify turnaround and ask about record complexity upfront.** Albuquerque properties often have solid Bernalillo County records, but older parcels in the metro area can be a nightmare — missing deeds, overlapping grants from territorial-era land transfers, or boundaries that were never formally recorded. A simple survey might take 2–3 weeks. A complex one with title gaps can stretch to 6–8 weeks. Call ahead and describe your property's age and any known issues. Bad surprises cost time and money.

**Check if they carry E&O insurance and understand their liability.** Professional liability (Errors & Omissions) insurance protects you if something goes wrong. It's standard for any licensed surveyor worth hiring. Ask for proof — it's not rude, it's professional.

> **Pro Tip:** If you're buying property or refinancing, your lender or title company often has surveyor recommendations. Use them. They vet for reliability because bad surveys cost *them* money. That's a built-in quality filter.

## What to Expect

You're looking at $500–$5,000+ depending on property size, complexity, and survey type. A 1-acre residential boundary survey in a established neighborhood: $600–$1,200. A 5-acre parcel with deed gaps or a commercial ALTA/NSPS: $2,500–$5,000+. Turnaround is typically 3–6 weeks for standard work; rush jobs (common before closing dates) cost 20–30% more.

The process is straightforward: you provide a legal description or address, the surveyor researches county records and prior surveys, field-checks the boundary, and delivers a sealed plat (a formal map signed by the PLS). You'll need access to the property — ideally clear of vehicles and obstructions along the boundary line.

> **Reality Check:** If a quote seems suspiciously cheap (under $400 for a boundary survey), ask why. It usually means either they're inexperienced, they're cutting corners on research, or they haven't factored in record complexity. You're paying for licensure, liability, and accuracy — not just someone with a GPS unit.
