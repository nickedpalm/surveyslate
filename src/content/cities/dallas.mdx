## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Dallas

**Check the license first.** Texas requires all surveyors to hold a Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) license issued by the Texas Board of Professional Land Surveying. Verify it at the [TBPLS database](https://www.tbpls.texas.gov/). This isn't optional—it's the baseline. If they don't have it, move on.

**Match the survey type to your job.** A residential boundary survey runs $500–$1,500. An ALTA/NSPS title survey (the gold standard for commercial transactions and what title companies often demand) starts at $1,500–$3,500 depending on complexity. Topographic surveys, construction staking, and elevation certificates each have different scopes and costs. Know what you actually need before you call—it saves time and money.

**Ask about turnaround and current capacity.** Dallas's real estate market moves fast. A surveyor quoting three weeks for a standard boundary survey is either overbooked or sandbagging you. Ask for their typical turnaround on your specific survey type. Then ask if they're currently taking new clients. Overbooked firms will tell you; the good ones won't waste your time.

**Confirm they'll sign and seal the deliverable.** The surveyor's professional seal and signature are legally required on every survey. That seal is their liability. It matters. If they're outsourcing to a technician or subcontractor, make sure a licensed PLS is reviewing and sealing the final product—not just rubber-stamping it.

**Pro Tip:** Call at least two surveyors for any project over $2,000. Prices vary wildly depending on site difficulty, travel distance, and property size. You might find a $500 difference on the same job. Get a written scope and fee estimate before committing.

## What to Expect

A standard boundary survey in Dallas typically takes 1–3 weeks from start to finish, depending on the surveyor's backlog and whether your deed and property records are clean or messy. The process looks like this: you call with your address and reason for the survey; they pull your legal description and property history; they visit the site and measure the boundaries; they produce a plat (the drawn map of your property); you sign off; they file it (if required) and send you the sealed original and copies.

Costs scale with complexity. A clean residential lot in a subdivided neighborhood runs $500–$1,000. Rural properties, unusual boundary disputes, or multi-acre commercial sites can easily hit $3,000–$5,000+. ALTA/NSPS surveys (required for commercial mortgages and major transactions) include additional research and requirements, so budget $1,500 minimum and often closer to $3,000–$4,000.

**Reality Check:** Don't choose a surveyor based on the lowest quote. The cheapest option often means they're overloaded and your project will sit in a queue for six weeks. Mid-range pricing ($1,000–$1,500 for standard residential work) usually signals a firm that's busy enough to be competent and not so slammed that you'll age waiting for results.
