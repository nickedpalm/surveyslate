The difference between hiring someone who actually knows Louisiana property law and someone who's just winging it can cost you thousands in title disputes, construction delays, or worse — a closing that falls apart two days before closing. ## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in New Orleans

**Check their Louisiana PLS license first.** Every surveyor who signs and seals a survey in Louisiana must hold a Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) license issued by the Louisiana Professional Engineers and Land Surveyors Board. Verify it on their website before you call — it takes 30 seconds and saves you from hiring someone operating on an expired credential or out-of-state license they're not authorized to use here.

**Ask what survey type you actually need.** Most people say "I need a survey" and stop there. But a simple boundary survey ($500–$1,200) for a residential mortgage is a completely different job than an ALTA/NSPS title survey ($2,000–$5,000+) for commercial real estate or a property dispute. A good surveyor will ask questions about why you need it before quoting a price. If they don't, they're probably low-balling or cutting corners.

**Local experience matters more than you think.** New Orleans property records are a labyrinth — some parcels date back to Spanish colonial grants, others overlap due to decades of flood-zone redrawing. You want someone who's spent years untangling this specific mess, not a national firm treating your lot like it's somewhere in suburban [Phoenix](/locations/arizona/phoenix/). Ask how long they've been surveying in New Orleans and if they've handled disputes involving your neighborhood or property type.

**Request references and turnaround timelines upfront.** Reputable surveyors will give you contact info for recent clients (especially if your job is similar to theirs). Ask for a written timeline — New Orleans weather and permitting delays are real, but a pro knows how to account for them. If they promise 48 hours for a complex job, they're lying.

**Pro Tip:** Louisiana allows surveyors to hold multiple state licenses (common for firms working across the Gulf Coast). If your property straddles a state line or you're dealing with riparian rights along the Mississippi, ask if they're licensed in adjacent states. It can save you from hiring a second firm mid-project.

## What to Expect

Boundary surveys in New Orleans typically run $500–$1,500 for residential work and scale up based on lot size, accessibility, and record complexity. ALTA/NSPS surveys for commercial or investment property jump to $2,000–$5,000+. Most jobs take 2–4 weeks from order to delivery, though weather, public records delays, and site access can push that out. You'll get a sealed, signed survey document that holds up in court, with your surveyor's credentials right there on the stamp.

**Reality Check:** Don't confuse cheap with fast. If someone quotes you $300 for a survey that your neighbor paid $1,200 for, ask why. It's usually because they're cutting corners on research, field time, or they're unlicensed and pricing accordingly.

## Local Market Overview

New Orleans's real estate market moves fast — especially in historically undervalued neighborhoods seeing gentrification and investment. That velocity means surveyors here deal with title issues constantly: properties with clouded chains of title, wetland or flood-zone complications, and occasional disputes over historic boundary lines. You need someone who understands not just the technical side, but the regulatory landscape (FEMA flood zones, Louisiana DNR permitting, etc.). It's not just about measuring — it's about knowing the law.
