## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Raleigh

**Verify their North Carolina PLS license first.** This isn't optional. Check the North Carolina Board of Examiners for Surveyors and Mappers directly—don't trust the website claim alone. A Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) has passed the Fundamentals of Surveying (FS) exam, the Professional Land Surveyor (PLS) exam, and logged thousands of hours in the field. In North Carolina, that's the only credential that matters for boundary work and court testimony.

**Know what survey type you actually need.** Mortgage lenders typically want a boundary survey or ALTA/NSPS survey (the full-meal-deal title survey for commercial transactions). If you're building, you need construction staking. Flood-prone property? Elevation certificate. Too many people call a surveyor, don't know what they're asking for, and get quoted for the wrong job. Call with specifics: "I'm closing on a residential property in North Raleigh" or "We're subdividing 40 acres in Wake County." The surveyor will ask clarifying questions—that's the sign they've actually done this before.

**Price isn't standardized, and it shouldn't be.** A simple residential boundary survey in a developed neighborhood runs $500–$1,500. Complex work—multi-parcel property, unclear old deeds, dense commercial zones—easily hits $3,000–$5,000+. Urban Raleigh surveys often cost more than rural Wake County work because property records are denser and access is tighter. Call three firms, get three estimates, and be suspicious of anyone quoting sight-unseen.

**Ask about turnaround and local experience.** Raleigh's real estate market moves fast. Understand whether the surveyor can hit your closing deadline. Also ask about their experience with your specific area—North Raleigh developments have different title patterns than South Raleigh or unincorporated Wake County. A surveyor who's done 50 surveys in your subdivision knows the quirks.

**Pro Tip:** Ask if the surveyor is NSPS-certified (National Society of Professional Surveyors member) or holds a CST (Certified Survey Technician) credential. These aren't required, but they signal that the firm keeps up with standards and continuing education. It's the difference between a surveyor and a surveyor who actually cares.

## What to Expect

The survey process typically takes 1–3 weeks from start to final seal, depending on complexity and record research. Your surveyor will visit the property, mark boundaries with flags or paint, research deed records at the Wake County Register of Deeds, and produce a sealed plat that's legal evidence in North Carolina courts. Costs escalate if old deeds conflict, if neighbors dispute lines, or if the property has easements or encroachments that need mapping.

**Reality Check:** Surveyor fees are not negotiable in the way you might think. You're not haggling over labor rates—you're paying for licensure, liability insurance, and the fact that their signature and seal are legally binding. Lowball quotes often mean the surveyor is cutting corners on research or field time. It's not where you save $200.
