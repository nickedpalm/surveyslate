Finding a qualified land surveyor in Omaha isn't hard because they don't exist — it's hard because most people don't know what to look for until they're already in a property dispute or stalled at closing. ## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Omaha

**Verify licensure in Nebraska first.** Every surveyor who seals a document in Nebraska must hold a current PLS (Professional Land Surveyor) license issued by the Nebraska Board of Engineers and Architects. Check the board's roster or ask directly — a real surveyor will have it memorized. This isn't gatekeeping; it's the legal minimum.

**Match the survey type to your need.** A boundary survey for a residential property ($500–$1,500) is straightforward. An ALTA/NSPS title survey for commercial real estate or complex transactions ($2,500–$5,000+) requires different credentials and takes longer. Know which one you need before you call — it saves time and prevents sticker shock.

**Ask about local experience.** Omaha's property records, platting history, and covenant-based subdivisions (especially in West Omaha) have quirks. A surveyor who's been in the Omaha market for 10+ years knows where the old monuments are, where records are shaky, and which properties typically come with boundary headaches. That knowledge is worth money.

**Check NSPS membership and CST credentials.** The National Society of Professional Surveyors (NSPS) is the industry standard body. Membership signals ongoing education and professional standards. If the surveyor or their team includes a Certified Survey Technician (CST), you're working with someone who's passed a national exam — not just a state requirement. It matters for complex work.

**Pro Tip:** Don't choose based on price alone. A cheap survey that misses a boundary easement or skips verification with the county costs you ten times more in legal fees later. Ask for references, especially from title companies or attorneys who've used them. They know who does solid work.

## What to Expect

A basic residential boundary survey in Omaha typically runs $700–$1,500 and takes 1–3 weeks (depending on record complexity and weather). The surveyor locates existing monuments or sets new ones, measures distances and angles to establish your property line, and delivers a signed, sealed plat. You get one official document — treat it like a legal record.

More complex work — ALTA surveys, subdivision platting, topographic surveys for construction — costs more and takes longer. Budget $2,500–$5,000+ and plan for 3–6 weeks, especially if the county records are incomplete or the property straddles multiple historic plats.

**Reality Check:** Surveyors don't set your fence line for you. The survey tells you where the legal boundary is. If you want to build or fence, that's between you and a contractor. Also, if your neighbor's fence has been in the "wrong" place for 20+ years, you may have a legal issue (adverse possession) that a survey uncovers but doesn't solve. Have a real estate attorney on speed dial if that's your situation.
