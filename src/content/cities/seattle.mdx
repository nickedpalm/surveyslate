## How to Choose a Land Surveyor in Seattle

**Verify their Washington State PLS license first.** This isn't optional. Every land surveyor in Washington must be licensed as a Professional Land Surveyor (PLS)—it's the only credential that matters legally. Check the Washington Department of Licensing website if you're doing your own homework. If a surveyor won't mention their license number in the first conversation, that's your exit signal.

**Match the surveyor's specialty to your actual need.** A residential boundary survey (typically $500–$1,500) is straightforward, but ALTA/NSPS surveys for commercial transactions or title work ($2,000–$5,000+) demand specific expertise. Topographic surveys, elevation certificates, construction staking, and subdivision platting all require different skill sets. Ask what they've done in the past 12 months that's identical to your project.

**Ask about turnaround time before you ask about price.** Seattle's real estate market moves fast. A surveyor who can deliver in 5 business days is worth more than one who quotes $200 less but takes three weeks. Refinances, closings, and development timelines don't flex. Get the timeline in writing.

**Check if they're bonded and insured.** Professional liability insurance and bonds protect you if something goes wrong—and they indicate a surveyor who takes their license seriously enough to back their work legally. This is standard for anyone operating above a certain threshold, so if they hem and haw about it, move on.

**Pro Tip:** Local experience matters more than you think. Seattle has specific geodetic datums, municipal code quirks, and title history patterns. A surveyor who's been working in King County for five years will navigate title abstracts and municipal records faster than someone fresh from [Portland](/locations/oregon/portland/). Ask how long they've been surveying in the Seattle area specifically.

## What to Expect

The process typically starts with a scope call—you describe the property, the reason for the survey, and what you need delivered. The surveyor will research the title, existing surveys, and relevant municipal records (usually 3–7 days). Then they'll visit the property, measure, take shots, and produce the final plat or survey document signed and sealed by their PLS license—that seal is what makes it legally admissible and binding.

Residential boundary surveys usually take 1–2 weeks start to finish. Commercial ALTA/NSPS work often takes 2–4 weeks depending on title complexity and the need for third-party coordination (utilities, adjacent owner agreements, etc.).

**Reality Check:** Pricing online is often a trap. A surveyor who quotes $400 on their website usually means a simple, urban residential lot with clear title and existing monuments. Rural property, missing monuments, title disputes, or commercial complexity? Expect 2–3x that baseline. Get a written estimate after the scope call, not before.
